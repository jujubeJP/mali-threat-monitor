Pattern Analysis

Key insights along the timeline
P1
Large-scale military base assaults become routine (May–Aug 2025)

Dioura (41), Boulikessi (100), Timbuktu (60+), and Tessit (90) — roughly 290 Malian soldiers killed in four months. Targets were isolated central and northern garrisons. In August, JNIM placed Farabougou under effective control.

P2
From "military war" to "economic war" (Sep 2025 – Mar 2026)

JNIM declared a fuel blockade and systematically attacked fuel convoys from Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Guinea, and Niger. More than 300 fuel trucks were destroyed. In parallel, kidnappings of foreign nationals (UAE royals, Indian engineers) generated ransom revenue. The center of gravity shifted from the north to the western and southern borders.

P3
JNIM–FLA coordination and the fall of Kidal (April 2026)

On April 25, the two organizations launched their first-ever coordinated attack, striking Bamako, Kati, the airport, Kidal, Gao, Mopti, and Sevare simultaneously. Defense Minister Sadio Camara was killed in a suicide bombing. FLA took full control of Kidal. GI-TOC called it "the largest security shock since 2012."

P4
Total loss of the north and the encirclement of Bamako (May–Jun 2026)

The Africa Corps withdrew successively from Tessalit and Aguelhok, disappearing entirely from northern Mali by the end of May. The Intahaka gold mine fell to FLA. JNIM cut three of Bamako's six trunk roads; bread and rice prices in the capital doubled, transport costs tripled. In the center, a major offensive against Dozo militias killed more than 50 in May alone. In June a bus hit a mine on the Bamako–Kayes road, killing eight. The junta responded with a one-year ban on motorcycle imports.

Long-Range Pattern Analysis

What 14 years reveals
1
Violence diffuses from north → center → everywhere

In 2012–2014, the center of gravity was northern Mali (Gao / Timbuktu / Kidal). After the 2015 Algiers Accord, weight shifted to the center starting from Mopti. From 2019 onward, Mopti became the permanent leader; from 2020, expansion reached the southwest (Sikasso, Kayes, Koulikoro); by 2025–2026 the capital Bamako entered the active zone. The geographic footprint of violence has more than doubled in a decade.

2
Positive correlation between coups and violence

After the August 2020 and May 2021 coups, annual fatalities surged year over year. The junta — which justified itself in the name of "improved security" — intensified counter-civilian operations alongside the Wagner deployment, and 2022–2023 fatality counts were roughly ten times the 2016 level. OECD concluded: "the junta did not inherit a deteriorating situation; it accelerated it."

3
Tactical cycle: bases → civilians → economy → coordination

JNIM's tactics underwent four qualitative shifts in 14 years. (1) 2012–2014: territorial control (garrison assaults) → (2) 2015–2019: stoking communal violence in the center → (3) 2020–2023: three-way state vs. JNIM vs. ISGS contest → (4) 2024–2026: economic blockade and pressure on the capital plus coordination with FLA. Each cycle shifted to "lower cost, higher political effect" methods.

4
Total defeat of external interventions

France's Serval (2013–2014, temporary success) → Barkhane (2014–2022, withdrew after 8 years) → MINUSMA (2013–2023, evaluated as a "failure" and expelled after 10 years) → Wagner (2021–2025, declared "mission accomplished" and withdrew after 4 years) → Africa Corps (2025–, withdrew from the north within one year). Five successive external security frameworks have failed in succession, while JNIM's scale and geographic reach expanded with each period.

Data Handling

Confidence ranges and caveats

Sources: ACLED (full Mali coverage since 1997) annual figures, ACLED Year in Review / Conflict Watchlist / Africa Overview issues, OECD "Military coups, jihadism and insecurity in the Central Sahel" (2024), the UN MINUSMA final report, HRW, and ISS Africa analyses.

Granularity: The long-range view shows annual aggregates only (not event-level). Plotting ~12,000 individual events across 14 years would be cognitively unmanageable, so this is a deliberate macro-level design choice. For event-level precision analysis, use the "Recent 13 Months" tab.

Confidence intervals: 2012–2016 figures carry roughly ±15% uncertainty (media coverage was thinner and ACLED is still backfilling). 2017–2024 is approximately ±10%. 2025–2026 is approximately ±5%. The 2026 figure covers only the first six months, so the year-end total may be up to twice as large.

Phase boundaries: An analytical layer unique to this dashboard, set on qualitative shifts in strategy, actors, and geography. Local analysts may draw different boundaries (for example, treating the MINUSMA withdrawal as the PF/PG inflection point).

This dashboard is an OSINT-based review of publicly available sources. Coordinates and casualty figures include estimates as reported at the time.
